The Big 12 enters 2026 with something it has not had in years: two teams that genuinely scare national programs. Texas Tech and BYU are not conference-good. They are nationally good, and the rest of the league will spend the next four months trying to prove there is a third option.

There might be. Utah's new coach inherits a loaded roster. Colorado's recruiting class looks like what the program promised it would be. Iowa State has gone 10-win in back-to-back seasons without anyone east of Kansas paying much attention. But projecting those programs past the top two requires a combination of performance and attrition that is easier to wish for than predict.

## Texas Tech: the standard

The Red Raiders were the conference's best team last fall by most measures and open as the favorite to win it again, even with the quarterback situation in flux after Brendan Sorsby entered the supplemental draft. The portal solved that problem faster than most expected, with a high-ranking transfer stepping in with a spring to build chemistry. The defense has been the real separator — Tech held four conference opponents under 17 points last season — and that unit is almost entirely intact.

The knock on Tech heading into this season is that they have not beaten a team outside the top 30 in their biggest wins. That résumé will be tested in November when BYU and Utah both come to Lubbock. Win those, and the argument becomes harder to dispute.

## BYU: the challenger

The Cougars do not view themselves as challengers. Their message all spring was direct: they went 12-2 last year, they should have been in the playoff, and they will not be politely waiting for a bid in 2026. Bear Bachmeier is back. LJ Martin — the Big 12 Offensive Player of the Year — is back. Three starting offensive linemen are back.

The defensive coordinator changed, but the defensive talent did not. Ten starters return in a unit that gave up under 20 points per game. Coach Sitake turned down Penn State in December. He is not here to build toward something. He is here to win now.

## The field

**Utah** (preseason No. 22) begins a new coaching era under Morgan Scalley with legitimate expectations and legitimate questions. The skill positions are loaded. The offensive line needs to prove itself. If it does, the Utes could be a dark horse for a title game appearance.

**Colorado** (preseason No. 20) has finally caught up to its recruiting hype. The transfer portal additions this cycle were targeted and smart, filling specific holes rather than wholesale rebuilding for the fourth consecutive year. This is the year Deion Sanders' program becomes a genuine threat rather than a compelling storyline.

**Iowa State** (preseason No. 19) is quietly the Big 12's most consistent program not named Texas Tech or BYU. They do not recruit at the level of the top two, but they develop at a rate that closes the gap by October. Another 10-win season is realistic.

**Kansas State** and **TCU** both have enough to beat anyone on a given Saturday but probably not enough to sustain it over twelve conference games.

## Our stake in it

You already know which games matter here. Utah's road at BYU in the Holy War. BYU's trip to Lubbock in October. Utah's home stretch against the conference's bottom half that will determine whether Scalley's first season ends with a bowl invite or a Big 12 title game appearance.

Both teams are ranked. Both have a path. In this conference, that is all you need.
