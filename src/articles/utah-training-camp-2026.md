The last time Utah opened fall camp with a new head coach, it was 1994. Kyle Whittingham spent twenty years building something in Salt Lake City that, in its best moments, was among the most consistent programs in the country. Now Morgan Scalley inherits the structure, the culture, and the recruiting relationships — and he inherits the pressure that comes with being the man who follows a legend.

Scalley has never done this before. Neither, at the head coaching level, has almost anyone who matters been a first-year head coach and thrived immediately. But the 2026 Utes have enough returning talent that the transition doesn't have to be rocky, and the preseason ranking of #22 reflects a program the country expects to be good even as it changes hands.

What camp will decide is whether Utah can close the gap with the programs above them in the Big 12 standings.

## The quarterback situation is the situation

Devon Dampier transferred in from Saguaro via the portal and enters camp as the presumptive starter. He's a dual-threat senior who put up numbers at a smaller program and whose athleticism fits what Scalley wants to do offensively. The question that camp answers is whether Dampier can operate efficiently against Big 12 speed and make consistent decisions in compressed windows when the pass rush gets home.

Behind Dampier, Byrd Ficklin is a second-year player with a live arm who benefited from the previous staff's developmental patience. If the offense stalls early or Dampier struggles against conference-level competition, Ficklin is the pivot. The quarterback competition is real enough that both players will get meaningful camp reps.

This is the central tension of Utah's 2026 season. The offensive line is elite. The skill position talent is there. The defense returns starters at every level. The ceiling of this team is almost entirely a function of who is throwing the football.

## Fano and the offensive line are a foundation

Spencer Fano (6-6, 308) is a junior tackle who plays like a senior who's been in this system for six years. He is the best offensive lineman in the Big 12 not named Andrew Gentry, and there will be a legitimate debate about that designation before October. Behind him, Zereoue Williams and Alex Harrison provide veteran stability at center and on the second tackle, while Michael Mokofisi and Tanoa Togiai anchor the interior.

This line can run the ball behind anyone who gives them a lead. They can protect the pocket for anyone who can make reads on schedule. Their primary limitation is what it's been for two years: the fourth and fifth blockers in the rotation are younger and less proven than the starters.

The offensive backfield has real talent. Wayshawn Parker transferred in and is a between-the-tackles runner with size and contact balance. Kyri Shoels is the best receiver Utah has had since Britain Covey in terms of short-area quickness and RAC ability. Tobias Merriweather (6-5, 200) gives the Utes a legitimate size mismatch on the outside.

## A defense that should carry its weight

Morgan Scalley built his reputation as a defensive coordinator. His 4-2-5 system prioritizes safety depth and safety intelligence, and this year's group has both. Rabbit Evans returns after a graduate transfer season in which he was among the better safeties in the conference. Nate Ritchie has been steady for three years. The linebacker corps, led by Trey Reynolds and Levani Damuni, has experience and physicality.

The defensive line concern is edge pass rush. Ethan Day, Paul Fitzgerald, and Lance Holtzclaw are all graduate seniors who have been part of this program for a long time. They have produced in the system. The question is whether any of them can produce *enough* against the better offensive lines in the Big 12 when the game is on the line in the fourth quarter.

Kash Dillon (RS-SO, 6-5) is the developmental player who could change that equation. If he takes the step in camp that the coaches believe he can, Utah's defensive line becomes a strength rather than a question. If he doesn't, the Utes will need to make up the difference with coverage and scheme.

## What Scalley needs to establish

A new head coach needs to establish one thing above all others in year one: that the program responds to him. Not just to the returning staff or to the institutional momentum from Whittingham's tenure, but specifically to the direction and culture that Scalley sets.

Fall camp is when that starts. How the Utes practice, how they handle adversity when installations go wrong, and how they respond to the inevitable growing pains of a transition — those things will be on display before the season begins.

The Holy War game is on the schedule in November. That game, with BYU entering it at #13 or better and with real playoff implications, may be the biggest regular-season game Utah has played in years. Year One for Morgan Scalley could have a very large final exam.

Fall camp starts now. Let's see what this team is made of.
