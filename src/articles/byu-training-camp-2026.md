Fall camp in Provo opened under the exact conditions BYU has spent three years building toward: a legitimate quarterback, an elite offensive line, a returning Big 12 Offensive Player of the Year in the backfield, and a defense that finished top-five in the conference in points allowed. The Cougars enter the 2026 season ranked 13th nationally and with legitimate designs on both a Big 12 title and a College Football Playoff berth.

Kalani Sitake has been patient. Now the bill has come due.

## The Bear question isn't really a question

Bear Bachmeier looked like a sophomore who had played 14 college football games last season, which is exactly what he was. He made some throws that nobody else on the BYU depth chart could have made. He also made some decisions that a sophomore makes when the game speeds up and the stakes get uncomfortable. The Cougars won twelve games anyway.

The growth coaches are watching for in fall camp is situational: how does Bachmeier manage the pocket on third and medium, what does his pre-snap processing look like in the red zone, and has the deep ball accuracy that showed flashes in the second half of the season held up through the offseason? Early camp reports suggest yes on all three counts.

The bigger change might be his relationship with the offense. Aaron Roderick's system rewards quarterbacks who can distribute quickly and trust their first read, and Bachmeier showed a tendency last year to hold the ball and try to create. With a full offseason of reps, the expectation in Provo is that the offense will operate faster, with more pre-snap communication and less improvisation.

His brother Tiger, back for his senior year at receiver, doesn't hurt.

## The line is the reason

Five seniors anchoring an offensive line that allowed the fewest sacks per game in the Big 12 last season is not an accident. Andrew Gentry (6-8, 315), Trevin Ostler, Kyle Sfarcioc, Bruce Mitchell, and Sonny Makasini have started together for two seasons. They know each other's tendencies, they know opposing defensive schemes, and they have the collective intelligence that only comes from game experience against good competition.

This is the kind of offensive line that defines programs, and it defines what BYU is right now: a team that controls the line of scrimmage and builds games from the inside out. LJ Martin ran for 1,300 yards behind this group last season. Martin is back for his senior year with a legitimate case for All-Big 12 first team honors before he's played a single snap of the 2026 season.

The concern — and there is one — is depth. When Gentry and Mitchell and Ostler graduate after this season, BYU's offensive line becomes a significant reconstruction project. But that's a 2027 problem. For 2026, this group is the program's biggest competitive advantage.

## What the defense needs to prove

The offense has a roadmap. The defense is operating with more questions.

Coordinator Kelly Poppinga returns six starters, including linebacker Isaiah Glasker (6-5, 240), who may be the most physically imposing linebacker in the Big 12, and safety Faletau Satuala (6-4, 215), who changed games last season with range and physicality that's unusual for a junior. The interior defensive line is deep and experienced, with Keanu Tanuvasa and Justin Kirkland both back for their final seasons.

The question marks are at cornerback and in the pass rush. Evan Johnson returns on the boundary and is a legitimate cover corner, but the depth behind him is young. Tausili Akana has the tools to emerge as a genuine pass rusher off the edge, but he's never been the primary option. The improvement of the pass rush and the development of the secondary depth will largely determine whether BYU can beat a team like Texas Tech or a national power in a late-season game when the quarterback has time and the field is compressed.

## Schedule and expectations

BYU opens September 5 at home. The Big 12 schedule includes games at Colorado, at Iowa State, and a late-season home game against Utah that everyone in Provo and Salt Lake City is already circling on their calendars.

The Cougars are positioned to win the Big 12. They have the quarterback, the line, the backfield, and the institutional confidence that comes from winning twelve games. Whether they can get to fourteen is the question that fall camp starts to answer.

The answers begin now.
